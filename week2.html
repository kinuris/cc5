The Challenge of Business Coordination

Imagine trying to coordinate a group project where each team member works in isolation, using different file formats, never sharing updates, and communicating only when absolutely necessary. The result would likely be duplication of effort, conflicting information, and a final product that lacks coherence. Unfortunately, this scenario describes how many businesses operated for decades—and some still do today.

This week, we explore how successful businesses manage the flow of information by breaking down internal barriers called "information silos" to create a single, unified view of the company's operations. This transformation allows everyone, from front-line workers to the CEO, to work from the same, up-to-date data, dramatically improving efficiency and decision-making.

We'll examine how enterprise systems serve as the technological backbone that enables this coordination, and how different levels of management use integrated information to make better decisions. By the end of this lesson, you'll understand why information integration is not just a technical challenge, but a fundamental business strategy.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • • Explain how business processes coordinate work across departments and the role of information in this coordination
  • • Identify the problems caused by information silos and their impact on business efficiency
  • • Understand how enterprise applications break down silos to create integrated business operations
  • • Differentiate between the information needs of operational, middle, and senior managers

The Foundation: Business Processes

Before we can understand why information silos are problematic, we need to grasp how businesses actually operate. At their core, businesses run on processes—structured sequences of activities designed to accomplish specific goals.

🔄 Business Process: The Coordination Challenge

A Business Process is a series of related steps required to produce a product or service. Most business processes span multiple departments, requiring careful coordination and information sharing to function effectively.

Example: When you order a product online, the process involves sales (taking your order), accounting (verifying payment), and warehouse operations (packing and shipping). Each step depends on information from the previous steps.

For this process to work efficiently, information must flow seamlessly between departments. Sales needs real-time inventory data, accounting needs order details, and the warehouse needs customer shipping information. When this information flow breaks down, the entire process suffers.

The Problem: Information Silos

Historically, many organizations developed separate information systems for each department or function. While this approach seemed logical—letting each department optimize its own operations—it created a significant problem: information silos.

🏗️ Information Silos: Barriers to Coordination

Information Silos are created when each department has its own separate system, trapping data within departmental boundaries and preventing effective communication across the organization.

Result: Departments operate with incomplete or outdated information, leading to inefficiency, errors, and missed opportunities for coordination.

💥 Operational Problems

  • • Selling products that are out of stock
  • • Shipping orders to outdated addresses
  • • Duplicate data entry across systems
  • • Delayed processing due to manual handoffs

📊 Strategic Problems

  • • Managers making decisions with incomplete data
  • • Inability to get organization-wide performance metrics
  • • Slow response to market changes
  • • Difficulty identifying cross-departmental trends

Real-World Impact: A study by IDC found that knowledge workers spend 2.5 hours per day searching for information, and 90% of large companies struggle with data silos that prevent effective decision-making. The cost of poor data quality and silos is estimated at $3.1 trillion annually in the US alone.

The Solution: Enterprise Applications

The solution to information silos lies in enterprise applications—large-scale systems designed to integrate data from across the organization into a single, shared database. These systems create what information professionals call a "single source of truth."

🏢 Enterprise Applications: Breaking Down Barriers

Enterprise Applications are information systems that span functional areas, execute business processes across the firm, and include all levels of management. They serve as the digital backbone that enables coordinated, organization-wide operations.

Key Benefit: All departments access the same real-time data, eliminating inconsistencies and enabling rapid, informed decision-making across the organization.

🔄 Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP)

The central nervous system for internal operations, integrating core business processes like finance, human resources, manufacturing, and supply chain management.

Example: SAP, Oracle ERP Cloud - When sales enters an order, inventory is automatically updated, accounting records the transaction, and production planning adjusts schedules—all in real-time.

👥 Customer Relationship Management (CRM)

Integrates all customer-facing processes and data, providing a 360-degree view of customer interactions across sales, marketing, and service departments.

Example: Salesforce, Microsoft Dynamics - Customer service can see sales history, marketing can track campaign responses, and sales can access support tickets—all from one system.

🚚 Supply Chain Management (SCM)

Integrates data shared with suppliers, distributors, and logistics partners to optimize the flow of materials and products from source to customer.

Example: When inventory drops below reorder points, the system automatically generates purchase orders to suppliers, optimizing timing and quantities based on demand forecasts.

💡 The Integration Advantage

With enterprise applications, when a customer places an order online, the system can instantly check inventory availability, verify credit, schedule production if needed, update accounting records, and notify the warehouse—all automatically and in real-time. This level of integration was impossible with siloed systems.

Information for Every Level of Management

Once data is integrated through enterprise systems, different levels of management can access and use this information in ways that align with their specific decision-making responsibilities. The key insight is that different managers need different views of the same underlying data.

⚡ Operational Managers

Transaction Processing Systems (TPS)

Use live, detailed data for daily operational tasks and routine decision-making.

Question: "What happened today?"
Example: Shift supervisor checking hourly production rates, quality metrics, and machine downtime.

📊 Middle Managers

Management Information Systems (MIS)

Use summarized reports and dashboards to monitor departmental performance and make tactical decisions.

Question: "How are we performing?"
Example: Regional sales manager reviewing monthly sales trends, comparing actual vs. budget performance.

🎯 Senior Managers

Executive Support Systems (ESS)

Use high-level dashboards and analytics for strategic planning and long-term decision-making.

Question: "What should our strategy be?"
Example: CEO reviewing market share trends, competitive analysis, and financial projections.

🔄 The Information Transformation Process

Notice how the same underlying data serves different purposes as it moves up the management hierarchy:

  • Raw transactions → Individual sales records, customer interactions, production events
  • Operational summaries → Daily/weekly reports, exception reports, performance metrics
  • Strategic insights → Trend analysis, competitive intelligence, market forecasts

Summary & Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Business Processes: Successful businesses operate through coordinated processes that span multiple departments, requiring seamless information flow to function effectively.
  • ✓ Information Silos: Departmental isolation creates significant operational and strategic problems, preventing organizations from achieving their full potential.
  • ✓ Enterprise Integration: Enterprise applications (ERP, CRM, SCM) break down silos by creating a single source of truth that enables real-time coordination across the organization.
  • ✓ Management Hierarchy: Different levels of management require different views of the same integrated data—from detailed operational transactions to high-level strategic insights.

Key Terms

Business Process

A series of related steps required to produce a product or service, typically spanning multiple departments and requiring coordinated information flow.

Information Silos

Barriers created when departments use separate systems, trapping data within functional boundaries and preventing organization-wide coordination.

Enterprise Applications

Large-scale information systems that integrate data and processes across functional areas to create a unified view of organizational operations.

Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP)

Integrated systems that manage core business processes including finance, human resources, manufacturing, and supply chain operations.

Customer Relationship Management (CRM)

Systems that integrate all customer-facing processes and data to provide a comprehensive view of customer interactions and relationships.

Supply Chain Management (SCM)

Systems that integrate data and processes shared with external partners to optimize the flow of materials and products from suppliers to customers.

Transaction Processing Systems (TPS)

Systems that provide operational managers with detailed, real-time data for day-to-day decision-making and routine operational tasks.

Management Information Systems (MIS)

Systems that provide middle managers with summarized reports and performance metrics for tactical decision-making and departmental oversight.
